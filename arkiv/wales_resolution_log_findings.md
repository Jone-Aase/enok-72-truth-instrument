### Column Headers (Summary Row)
The daily summary row has these columns:
1. **Course**
2. **Distance**
3. **S** (speed, knots)
4. **F** (fathoms - sounding depth)
5. **Latit. D.R.** (Latitude by Dead Reckoning)
6. **Latit. Observ'd** (Observed Latitude - from sextant, sun's meridian altitude)
7. **Long. D.R.** (Longitude by Dead Reckoning)
8. **Long by K** ← **KENDALL (K1) CHRONOMETER LONGITUDE** ←
9. **Long by A** (Longitude by Arnold watches - companion chronometers)
10. **Long by Obs** (Longitude by Lunar Observations)
11. **Barom't** (Barometer)
12. **Thermom't A** / **B** (Thermometers)

### KEY FINDING: "Long by K" = Longitude by Kendall K1 Chronometer
The column explicitly labeled **"Long by K"** refers to the **K1 watch** (Larcum Kendall's copy of Harrison's H4), which was carried on HMS Resolution. This is the chronometer that Cook later famously praised as "our never failing guide."

### Evidence of K1 Chronometer Use
1. **"Long by K"** column is explicitly present and **filled in** with longitude values
2. The K1 (Kendall) watch enabled Wales to calculate longitude from Greenwich time
3. The log shows the ship is at approximately **54-55° South latitude** in December 1772 — deep in the Southern Ocean
4. This is precisely the period where they were probing toward Antarctica, attempting to cross the Antarctic Circle (which they first crossed on 17 January 1773)

### Comparison with Cook's FIRST Voyage
On Cook's first voyage (1768-1771 on HMS Endeavour), **no chronometer was carried**. The longitude column in Banks/Cook's logs was either:
- Filled only by **lunar distance observations** (laborious, error-prone)
- Or left blank when conditions prevented lunar observations

On the **second voyage** (1772-1775), with the K1 chronometer:
- **"Long by K"** column is filled daily with longitude from the watch
- **"Long by A"** column also present (Arnold chronometers Nos. 82 and 176)
- Multiple methods used simultaneously for comparison and verification
- This transformed navigation accuracy enormously

### Significance
This logbook page (RGO 14/58, f.11v, December 12-13, 1772) is direct archival evidence that:
1. William Wales **recorded longitude daily from the K1 Kendall chronometer**
2. The K1 is explicitly labeled in the column header as **"Long by K"**
3. They were at ~54°S in the Southern Ocean, well below the Tropic of Capricorn
4. This was approximately 6 months into the voyage (left Plymouth July 13, 1772)
5. They were heading south toward their first Antarctic Circle crossing (January 17, 1773)
